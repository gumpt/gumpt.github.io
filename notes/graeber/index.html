Graeber's Oeuvre

If I could write like anyone, I would write like David Graeber.

I say that based on how the first writing of his in The Baffler I came across1 wormed its way into my brain, a reference whenever I had to spend too much of my day doing “busywork,” figuring out why the tickets in the bug tracker had a cyclical reference making the root of the cause undecipherable, filling out status updates in three separate but Very Important places, listening to friends describe what ran them out of academia.

I say that based on his political analysis, having referenced his analysis of British elections post-Brexit more often than anything else when trying to describe fundamental political problems in the United States to anyone else, or predict (somewhat accurately) what would happen in 2020.

I say that because he had a knack for calling out bullshit and naming it such, going semi-viral for calling the current state of work an epidemic of Bullshit Jobs, later expanding the thesis into a book, writing so many sentences that made me want to yell and scream and stamp my feet that someone finally knows what they’re talking about, has their finger on the pulse but the larger picture in mind, an acute and terminal understanding of the world around us.

The answer clearly isn’t economic: it’s moral and political. The ruling class has figured out that a happy and productive population with free time on their hands is a mortal danger (think of what started to happen when this even began to be approximated in the ’60s). And, on the other hand, the feeling that work is a moral value in itself, and that anyone not willing to submit themselves to some kind of intense work discipline for most of their waking hours deserves nothing, is extraordinarily convenient for them.2

I say that because his humor comes through despite this subject matter, how bureaucratic structures in our government are reflected in our languages and thoughts, how The Left was fighting (and losing!) the wrong battles, how there would never be The Revolution, the one moment where you could point to a Before and an After, clearly demarcated by drastic improvements in people’s lives, but that we need to work every single day to try to make the world around us what it needs to be.

Someone once figured out that the average American will spend a cumulative six months of life waiting for traffic lights to change. I don’t know if similar figures are available for how long it takes to fill out forms, but it must be at least as long. No population in the history of the world has spent nearly so much time engaged in paperwork.1

All of his books, articles, blog posts, interviews, tweets, coughs and other gutteral utterances, are worth reading closely.

  1. A footnote is required to note how wonderful the title is: Of Flying Cars and the Declining Rate of Profit. ↩︎

  2. https://www.strike.coop/bullshit-jobs/ ↩︎
